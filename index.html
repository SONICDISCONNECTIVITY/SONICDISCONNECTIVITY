The Project

What does global dis:connectivity sound like?

This project treats the city of Tunis as a sonic laboratory — an experimental site where the dis:connectivity of global processes becomes acoustically perceptible. Through field recordings from contrasting urban zones, it investigates how the same forces that produce connection and acoustic management in one part of the city simultaneously produce exposure, noise, and improvised substitution in another.

The city is not approached as a backdrop. It is the laboratory itself: a space where elements of global integration and disintegration can be isolated, juxtaposed, and activated as investigative tools. Listening is the method. The soundscape is the evidence.

The recordings ask: who has access to engineered silence, and who remains acoustically exposed? What global processes are registered in everyday urban sound — and what does their absence tell us about the limits of connection?

Zone III  ·  Recorded July 2026

Sidi Bou Said

Engineered Silence · Acoustic Privilege

The quieted northern suburb. No motorbikes. Birdsong, café terraces, the sea from a distance. This silence is not natural — it is produced through urban planning, property values, and the physical exclusion of informal commerce. Recorded at the same time of day as Hafseya, it is the acoustic form of the same global forces that generate the noise elsewhere.

Theoretical Framework

The Concepts

Roland Wenzlhuemer

Dis:connectivity

The world does not shrink uniformly through globalisation — it warps. The same infrastructure that connects one zone simultaneously disconnects another. Dis:connectivity names this turbulent, mutually constitutive relationship: the silence of Sidi Bou Said and the acoustic density of Hafseya are not two separate phenomena. They are produced together, by the same forces, at the same moment.

Homi K. Bhabha

The Third Space

In postcolonial urban contexts, most places exist neither fully inside global circuits nor fully outside them. Bhabha's Third Space names this permanent structural condition — hybrid, unstable, generative. The working-class neighborhoods of Tunis are not transitioning toward full integration. They permanently inhabit the in-between: produced by global forces but not fully served by them.

AbdouMaliq Simone

People as Infrastructure

Where formal infrastructure is inadequate, people build their own. Vendors, craftsmen, louage drivers, and market workers create collective improvised systems that substitute for what formal development does not provide. In Tunis, the acoustic density of working-class streets is not disorder — it is people functioning as infrastructure. The noise is the sound of collective agency in the Third Space.

Steven Feld

Acoustemology

Acoustemology — acoustic epistemology — is the claim that listening is a form of knowing, not merely a form of hearing. Sound does not illustrate knowledge produced elsewhere; it produces its own knowledge. When we listen to Tunisian soundscapes as an experimental method, we are not decorating an argument already made in text. We are conducting an inquiry that text alone cannot perform.
